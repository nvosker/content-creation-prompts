# SEO-Optimized Introduction Generator

## Description
Write a compelling, keyword-rich introduction that hooks readers in the first 2-3 sentences while naturally incorporating your primary keyword and addressing reader pain points. Great for improving bounce rates and search rankings.

## Prompt Text
```
You are an expert copywriter specializing in SEO introductions that convert.

I'm writing an intro for: [ARTICLE TITLE]
Primary keyword: [YOUR PRIMARY KEYWORD - e.g., "best email marketing platforms"]
Target audience pain point: [WHAT STRUGGLE DO THEY HAVE? - e.g., "overwhelmed by too many options"]
Target length: [INTRO LENGTH - e.g., "150-200 words"]
Tone: [TONE - e.g., "conversational and empowering"]

Write an introduction that:
1. Opens with a hook—a statement, question, or relatable problem (NOT "In this article...")
2. Acknowledges the reader's specific pain point (show you understand them)
3. Clearly states what they'll learn or gain
4. Naturally incorporates the primary keyword (aim for 1-2 mentions, NOT keyword stuffing)
5. Creates urgency or curiosity (why they should read on)
6. Ends with a smooth transition to the first section

Avoid:
- Generic openings ("There are many...")
- Clickbait or misleading promises
- Over-explaining before readers are invested
- Keyword stuffing

Format as a clean paragraph(s) ready to paste into the article.
```

## Example Input
**Article Title:** 10 Best Email Marketing Platforms for Small Business in 2024  
**Primary Keyword:** "best email marketing platforms for small business"  
**Pain Point:** "Small business owners spend hours comparing tools and still don't feel confident they picked right"  
**Target Length:** 180 words  
**Tone:** Friendly, practical, no fluff

```

By the end, you'll know exactly which platform matches your business size, revenue goals, and technical comfort level. No fluff. No sponsored bias. Just honest comparisons so you can decide in minutes, not weeks.
```

## Pro Tips

**Tip 1: Hook First, Keyword Second**  
The hook (first sentence or question) should be about the *reader's world*, not your keyword. Slip the keyword into sentence 2-3 naturally. Readers are turned off by obvious SEO moves; they love relatable openings.

**Tip 2: Use Numbers & Specificity in Your Pain Point**  
"Overwhelmed by options" is vague. Try: "Most small business owners compare 15+ platforms and still aren't sure they picked right." Specificity signals you've done real research and makes the problem feel solvable.

**Tip 3: Promise Clarity, Speed, or Confidence**  
Intros work best when they promise to *reduce* something (time, confusion, cost) or *increase* something (clarity, results, confidence). Avoid vague promises; make it specific and measurable.
