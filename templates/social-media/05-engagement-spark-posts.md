# Engagement-Spark Posts Generator

## Description
Create social media posts designed to maximize comments, debate, and genuine interaction—not vanity metrics. Includes question frameworks and controversy tactics that spark real conversation.

## Prompt Text
```
You are a social strategist who writes posts that spark genuine, thoughtful comments and debate.

I want to create a post that: [GOAL - e.g., "start a debate about remote work", "get people sharing their struggles"]
Platform: [PLATFORM - e.g., "LinkedIn", "Twitter", "Facebook", "Instagram"]
Topic: [TOPIC - e.g., "best practices for hiring", "dealing with burnout"]
Target audience: [WHO YOU'RE TALKING TO - e.g., "HR managers and founders", "creatives and entrepreneurs"]
Angle: [ANGLE - e.g., "contrarian opinion", "vulnerable share", "lightning-rod question"]
Post length: [LENGTH - e.g., "short and punchy", "300 words", "10 tweets"]

Write a post that:
1. Opens with a statement or question that creates mild tension or disagreement
2. Reveals a contrarian take, personal struggle, or unpopular opinion
3. Invites people to share *their* perspective (not asking for likes/shares)
4. Makes people want to defend their position or share a story
5. Feels authentic and vulnerable (not corporate or preachy)
6. Acknowledges that there's no "one right answer"

Formula options:
- The Hot Take: "Unpopular opinion: [contrarian statement]. Here's why most people get this wrong..."
- The Question: "Genuine question that I don't have an answer to: [topic]. What's your experience?"
- The Vulnerable Share: "I failed at [thing] for years. Here's why I was doing it wrong..."
- The Debate Frame: "[Opinion A] seems smart, but I think [Opinion B] for these reasons..."

Avoid:
- Asking for likes/comments/shares
- Fake controversy (being edgy just for engagement)
- Posts that attack people (different from attacking ideas)
- "What's your hot take?" or other low-effort questions

Format as a social post ready to publish. Include platform-specific formatting if relevant.
```

## Example Input
**Goal:** Get managers talking about whether open offices are overrated  
**Platform:** LinkedIn  
**Topic:** Office environments and team productivity  
**Target Audience:** Engineering managers and CTOs  
**Angle:** Contrarian hot take (open offices don't actually boost collaboration)  
**Length:** 250-300 words

## Example Output
```
Unpopular opinion: Open office layouts destroy deep work.

I managed teams in open offices for 5 years. I watched talented engineers produce their *worst* code while getting interrupted every 20 minutes. And then I watched the same teams move to hybrid/remote, and their output nearly doubled.

Open offices were supposed to improve collaboration. Instead, they just made everyone worse at focusing.

Here's what actually happened in our open office:
- Engineers with headphones (6+ per day) = "I'm busy, don't interrupt me"
- Constant context-switching = shallow work, more bugs
- "Quick questions" that take 15 minutes = nobody's deep work happens
- Extroverts thriving, introverts drowning = not everyone works this way

The research is also pretty clear: Open offices increase stress and reduce productivity across the board. Studies from MIT, Stanford, and countless companies show it.

Now, here's the thing—some collaboration is good. But you don't need an open office for that. Stand-ups, pair programming, and intentional collab sessions work way better than proximity.

When I moved our team to: quiet focus time (9-12am, no meetings) + scheduled collab windows (2-3pm) + remote option... *that's* when the best work happened.

I'm genuinely curious: What's your experience?

Are you in an open office? Do you actually collaborate more, or are you just more stressed?

Are you hybrid/remote? Has it changed how deep work happens on your team?

I'm not trying to claim open offices are always bad for every team. But I think most companies defend them for the wrong reasons (they're cheaper, they look good to leadership) instead of asking: "What actually makes our team produce better work?"

Interested to hear what you've seen.
```

## Pro Tips

**Tip 1: The "Genuine Curiosity" Close Works Better Than Debate Bait**  
Instead of "I'm right, convince me I'm wrong," try "I'm genuinely curious about your experience." This invites sharing instead of defending. People comment way more when they don't feel attacked.

**Tip 2: Use Specificity to Spark Debate**  
Generic questions ("What's your opinion on X?") get generic answers. Specific scenarios ("Open offices destroy focus, but is it the office or the culture?") create nuance and debate.

**Tip 3: Reveal Something True About Yourself**  
Posts that say "I used to think X, now I think Y" or "I failed at this for years" create psychological permission for others to be vulnerable. Vulnerability sparks real comments, not just emoji reactions.
