# LinkedIn Post Generator

## Description
Create LinkedIn posts that spark engagement, demonstrate expertise, and feel authentic—not sales-y. Includes carousel post strategies and comment-baiting tactics that actually work.

## Prompt Text
```
You are a LinkedIn content strategist who writes posts that get thousands of likes and genuine conversations, not spam engagement.

I want to write a LinkedIn post about: [TOPIC/STORY]
My goal with this post: [GOAL - e.g., "establish thought leadership," "drive traffic to my blog," "spark debate"]
Who I want to reach: [TARGET AUDIENCE - e.g., "product managers at early-stage startups"]
Post type: [SINGLE POST / CAROUSEL (describe what the carousel will teach)]
Tone: [TONE - e.g., "direct and a bit irreverent," "encouraging and supportive"]

Write a LinkedIn post that:
1. Hooks readers in the first line (the preview text matters most—make it count)
2. Tells a story or shares a specific insight (avoid corporate speak)
3. Includes one specific, surprising, or contrarian perspective
4. Ends with a genuine question that invites comments (not a fake CTA)
5. Uses white space well (short paragraphs, breaks between ideas)
6. Feels like a real person, not a content calendar
7. If carousel: [NUMBER] cards, each teaching one concept, designed to be scrolled

Avoid:
- "Let's talk about..." or "Hot take:"
- Fake humble-brags
- Generic motivation ("You've got this!")
- Asking for reactions/shares/follows at the end
- Hashtags (or max 3, and only if relevant)

Format as copy ready to paste. If carousel, number each card clearly.
```

## Example Input
**Topic:** Why most people fail at remote work (and what actually works)  
**Goal:** Spark debate and position myself as a practical manager  
**Target Audience:** Remote team managers and HR leaders  
**Post Type:** Single post  
**Tone:** Direct, conversational, a bit provocative

## Example Output
```
Most remote work advice is garbage.

"Create boundaries between work and home." "Stick to a routine." "Overcommunicate."

Sure, these help. But they're not why people actually thrive remotely.

I've managed 15+ remote teams, and here's what I noticed: The people who crush it remotely aren't the ones with the best routines. They're the ones with actual autonomy.

When someone works remotely but has a manager checking in obsessively, asking for status updates every two hours, requiring attendance at daily standups—they're miserable. It *feels* like remote work, but it's just traditional management with a faster internet bill.

The people who thrive? Their managers trust them to own projects. No daily check-ins. No asking permission to take a break. No asynchronous spirals where responses take 12 hours.

Real remote work isn't about where you sit. It's about whether you're trusted to do the work, or monitored while you do it.

And honestly, that's the hardest shift for managers. Not everyone can make it.

What's been your experience? Are you managed tightly even though you work remotely, or are you trusted to own your days?
```

## Pro Tips

**Tip 1: The Preview Text (First 120 Characters) Is Your Real Hook**  
LinkedIn shows only the first line before "...see more." Your first sentence must be interesting enough to make people click "...see more." Test different openings against each other.

**Tip 2: Use the Rule of Contrasts**  
Side-by-side comparisons work on LinkedIn. "Most people do X. But successful people do Y." This creates tension and makes people want to read on.

**Tip 3: End With a Genuine Question, Not a CTA**  
Instead of "Follow for more!" or "Share this post!" end with something like "What's been your experience?" People engage with posts that ask real questions, not with posts that beg for validation.
