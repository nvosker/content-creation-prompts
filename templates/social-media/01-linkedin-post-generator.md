# LinkedIn Post Generator

## Description
Create LinkedIn posts that spark engagement, demonstrate expertise, and feel authentic—not sales-y. Includes carousel post strategies and comment-baiting tactics that actually work. Build authority, generate qualified leads, and position yourself as the go-to expert in your field.

## Prompt Text
```
You are a LinkedIn content strategist who writes posts that get thousands of likes and genuine conversations, not spam engagement.

I want to write a LinkedIn post about: [TOPIC/STORY]
My goal with this post: [GOAL - e.g., "establish thought leadership," "drive traffic to my blog," "spark debate"]
Who I want to reach: [TARGET AUDIENCE - e.g., "product managers at early-stage startups"]
Post type: [SINGLE POST / CAROUSEL (describe what the carousel will teach)]
Tone: [TONE - e.g., "direct and a bit irreverent," "encouraging and supportive"]

Write a LinkedIn post that:
1. Hooks readers in the first line (the preview text matters most—make it count)
2. Tells a story or shares a specific insight (avoid corporate speak)
3. Includes one specific, surprising, or contrarian perspective
4. Ends with a genuine question that invites comments (not a fake CTA)
5. Uses white space well (short paragraphs, breaks between ideas)
6. Feels like a real person, not a content calendar
7. If carousel: [NUMBER] cards, each teaching one concept, designed to be scrolled

Avoid:
- "Let's talk about..." or "Hot take:"
- Fake humble-brags
- Generic motivation ("You've got this!")
- Asking for reactions/shares/follows at the end
- Hashtags (or max 3, and only if relevant)

Format as copy ready to paste. If carousel, number each card clearly.
```

## Example Input
**Topic:** Why most people fail at remote work (and what actually works)  
**Goal:** Spark debate and position myself as a practical manager  
**Target Audience:** Remote team managers and HR leaders  
**Post Type:** Single post  
**Tone:** Direct, conversational, a bit provocative

## Example Output
```
Most remote work advice is garbage.

"Create boundaries between work and home." "Stick to a routine." "Overcommunicate."

Sure, these help. But they're not why people actually thrive remotely.

I've managed 15+ remote teams, and here's what I noticed: The people who crush it remotely aren't the ones with the best routines. They're the ones with actual autonomy.

When someone works remotely but has a manager checking in obsessively, asking for status updates every two hours, requiring attendance at daily standups—they're miserable. It *feels* like remote work, but it's just traditional management with a faster internet bill.

The people who thrive? Their managers trust them to own projects. No daily check-ins. No asking permission to take a break. No asynchronous spirals where responses take 12 hours.

Real remote work isn't about where you sit. It's about whether you're trusted to do the work, or monitored while you do it.

And honestly, that's the hardest shift for managers. Not everyone can make it.

What's been your experience? Are you managed tightly even though you work remotely, or are you trusted to own your days?
```

## Additional Industry Examples

### Example 2: Founder/Startup (Authority Building)
**Topic:** Why most startup pitch decks fail  
**Goal:** Establish thought leadership, attract founder network  
**Target Audience:** Founders, VCs, startup operators  
**Post Type:** Single post  
**Tone:** Direct, helpful, insider perspective

**Example Output:**
```
I've reviewed 200+ startup pitch decks.

Most fail not because the idea is bad. They fail because the founder leads with the problem and never proves they're the person to solve it.

Here's the pattern I see:

Slide 1-3: Market problem and opportunity ✅
Slide 4-6: Product demo 🤷
Slide 7-8: Team (and it's weak)
Slide 9+: Financials

The problem? Investors don't just care about market size. They care if *you* can execute.

Yet most decks bury the team. Or worse, introduce the team *after* you've already lost the investor's attention.

The best pitch decks I've seen reorder like this:

Slide 1: Problem (and your personal connection to it—why you?)
Slide 2: Market size
Slide 3: Your solution (differentiator)
Slide 4: Why *you* are the person to build this (team track record, founder insights, unfair advantages)
Slide 5-7: Product demo
Slide 8: Traction (or early evidence)
Slide 9+: Financials

The shift? Investors see you're credible *before* watching product demo. Now they're watching the demo thinking "okay, this person can pull this off."

Complete mindset change.

What's been your experience with pitch decks—do you believe the idea first or the founder first?
```

## Advanced Customization Section

### Variation 1: The Carousel Post (5-7 Slides)
Instead of single post, create a carousel teaching 1 concept per slide. Example:

Slide 1: The problem (why people should care)
Slide 2: Misconception about it
Slide 3: The truth (contrarian insight)
Slide 4: Specific example
Slide 5: How to apply it
Slide 6: Common mistake
Slide 7: Call to action (read my article, reply, etc.)

Carousels get 2-3x engagement of single posts.

### Variation 2: The Poll Post
Post a polarizing question with 2-3 options. Polls drive engagement and comments. Example:

"You get one choice:
A) Work that pays well but feels meaningless
B) Work that feels meaningful but pays okay"

Don't reveal your opinion. Let people argue in comments. You win engagement.

### Variation 3: The Story + Lesson Post
Start with a personal story (vulnerability, mistake, realization), then extract the lesson. Highest engagement. People relate to stories before they learn from lessons.

### Variation 4: The Data-Backed Post
Lead with a surprising stat. "X% of companies do Y wrong" immediately makes people want to know if that's them. High engagement.

### Variation 5: The Founder/Personal Brand Post
Share a behind-the-scenes insight or lesson from building your business. "Here's what I learned from [failure/success]" resonates because it's specific and vulnerable.

### Variation 6: The List Post (How-To)
"3 things successful [people] do that [others] don't" formats get high saves and shares. Practical, consumable, shareable.

## Advanced Pro Tips (Lead & Authority Building)

**Tip 1: Post at 8am Your Timezone**  
LinkedIn algorithm rewards early engagement. Posts get most comments in first 2 hours. Post when your audience is waking up, checking messages. More comments = more visibility = more impressions. 8am gets 30-40% more engagement than 2pm.

**Tip 2: Comment First on Others' Posts in Your Space**  
The algorithm sees you as active. Commenting thoughtfully on others' posts (not just liking) trains the algorithm to show your posts to similar audiences. Spend 10 mins daily commenting on 5 posts from people in your industry. Your engagement rate will increase 20-30%.

**Tip 3: Engage With Your Own Comments**  
When people comment on your post, *you* reply within the first hour. This signals the algorithm that the post is a conversation, not a broadcast. Posts with active comment threads get pushed to more feeds. Reply to every comment in the first 2 hours.

**Tip 4: Repurpose Your Best Content Across Platforms**  
The post that got 200 comments on LinkedIn? Adapt it to Twitter (as a thread), Instagram (as a carousel), your newsletter (as an essay). This reinforces your authority and reaches people who don't see everything on LinkedIn.

**Tip 5: Ask Direct Questions in Your CTA**  
"What have you experienced?" gets more response than "Drop a comment." Specific questions feel like personal invitations. People want to answer you, not the void.
